The two rules that explain most file behavior

Welcome to a short walking tour. Before we step into the galleries, two spells run this whole museum:

  1. Lossy vs. lossless. Lossy formats (like JPG, MP3, most video) throw away details human senses notice least so files get smaller. You can’t get that detail back. Lossless formats (like PNG, FLAC, ZIP) squeeze without deleting—unpack and you get the exact original.
  2. Transparency (alpha). Some image formats can store see‑through pixels. JPEG cannot. If you save a transparent logo as JPG, the “empty” pixels fill with a color (usually white). That’s why you see a white box or pale halo when you place it on a dark background. Use PNG, WebP, or AVIF when you need transparency.

Images

Photos are made of pixels (raster). Logos and icons can also be vectors (math paths) that scale forever. Here’s how to choose.

Best for quick picks
  • Photos (no transparency): JPG, WebP, AVIF
  • Graphics/UI, needs crisp edges or transparency: PNG, SVG, WebP/AVIF
  • Animation: animated WebP or APNG (GIF only for simple/retro)
Gotchas
  • JPG has no transparency → white boxes/halos on cut‑outs.
  • PNG is lossless—great for logos, big for photos.
  • AVIF is tiny but slower to encode; keep a fallback if compatibility matters.

JPEG / JPG

Use for: photographs and complex scenes when you don’t need transparency.

Why it’s good: small files; universal support.

Trade‑offs: blocky/ringy artifacts at low quality; no alpha; white halos on cut‑outs.

Settings tip: quality ~75–85 for web; avoid re‑saving lossy files repeatedly.

PNG

Use for: logos, UI, screenshots, line art, anything needing true transparency.

Why it’s good: lossless; sharp edges; alpha channel.

Trade‑offs: often much larger than JPG for photos.

WebP

Use for: modern web images (both photos and graphics), including transparency and animation.

Why it’s good: smaller than JPG/PNG at similar quality; flexible.

Trade‑offs: encoding can be slower; older tools may lag.

AVIF

Use for: smallest high‑quality photos; supports transparency.

Why it’s good: excellent compression; great gradients with 10‑bit color.

Trade‑offs: slower to encode; ensure your audience/apps can open it (keep a fallback if needed).

GIF, APNG, Animated WebP

  • GIF: 256‑color limit; big files for real‑world footage; universal nostalgia.
  • APNG: full color + transparency; great for UI/illustrations.
  • Animated WebP: often the best size/quality for the web.

SVG (vector)

Use for: logos, icons, diagrams that must scale perfectly.

Why it’s good: tiny for simple art; infinite resolution; can be styled.

Trade‑offs: heavy filters/embeds can bloat; not for photos.

TIFF & RAW

Use for: archiving and editing workflows; print‑ready masters.

Audio

Quick picks
  • Universal sharing: MP3
  • Video soundtracks / streaming: AAC (M4A)
  • Speech at low bitrates: Opus
  • Editing / archive: WAV (PCM) or FLAC
Watch‑outs
  • Lossy (MP3/AAC/Opus) is not for re‑editing repeatedly.
  • WAV is huge; save it for masters/DAWs.

MP3 plays everywhere; 128–192 kbps covers most needs. AAC/M4A is more efficient, great for video. Opus shines for voice and streaming. WAV/AIFF are raw PCM for editing; FLAC/ALAC are lossless but smaller than WAV.

Video

Quick picks
  • Plays almost anywhere: MP4 (H.264 + AAC)
  • Smaller web files: WebM (VP9/AV1 + Opus)
  • Editing intermediates: MOV (ProRes), high‑bitrate MP4
Watch‑outs
  • Use yuv420p for compatibility.
  • AV1 is efficient but slower to encode; ensure playback support.

Containers (MP4, MKV, MOV, WebM) are boxes; codecs (H.264, VP9, AV1; AAC, Opus) are how content is compressed. For MP4/H.264, a good starting point is CRF ~23 and preset “faster/fast.” For WebM, VP9 is mature; AV1 is smallest at the cost of time.

Documents

Choose this
  • To edit together: DOCX
  • To share & print exactly: PDF
  • To publish on the web: HTML / Markdown
  • To read like a book: EPUB
Notes
  • PDF accessibility depends on proper tagging and real text.
  • DOCX layout can shift across systems; embed fonts if needed.

Data

Use this
  • Simple tables: CSV
  • APIs/configs: JSON
  • Spreadsheets: XLSX
  • Analytics: Parquet / Arrow
Pitfalls
  • CSV has no schema → commas/newlines/encodings can break things.
  • JSON is verbose; agree on a schema and field names.

Archives

ZIP is the default everyone can open. 7z squeezes smaller but needs a helper app on some systems. TAR.GZ/XZ are common on Unix for packaging + compression.

Quick decision trees

Images

  • Need transparency or razor‑sharp edges? → PNG/SVG (or WebP/AVIF with alpha)
  • Photo, no transparency? → JPG (fallback) or WebP/AVIF (smaller)
  • Animation? → Animated WebP/APNG (avoid big GIFs)

Audio

  • Share anywhere? → MP3
  • For video/streaming? → AAC (M4A)
  • Edit/archive? → WAV/FLAC

Video

  • Universal? → MP4 (H.264 + AAC)
  • Smaller web? → WebM (VP9/AV1)
  • Editing? → MOV/ProRes

Documents

  • Editable? → DOCX
  • Final/share/print? → PDF

Troubleshooting (fast fixes)

  • Logo shows a white box: Export as PNG/WebP/AVIF with transparency; don’t use JPG.
  • Animated image is huge: Use animated WebP or APNG instead of GIF.
  • Screenshot looks blurry: Save as PNG (JPG compression and chroma subsampling blur UI text).
  • Colors shift in print: Convert to the print shop’s profile; proof before mass print.
  • Excel ate leading zeros: That’s CSV. Quote fields or ship JSON/XLSX.

Glossary

  • Alpha: the transparency channel in images.
  • Container vs. Codec: the box vs. the compression method (for video/audio).
  • CRF: “constant rate factor,” a quality slider for many video encoders.
  • Chroma subsampling: storing color at lower resolution than brightness to save space.
  • Lossless vs. Lossy: whether compression deletes detail or not.
Remember the three questions:
  1. Do you need transparency or perfect edges?
  2. Do you want something editable or a final share?
  3. Who needs to open it, and on what devices?
